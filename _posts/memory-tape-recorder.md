A significant portion of the general population, including mental health professionals, holds the belief that memory functions similarly to a permanent tape recorder (Lynn et al., 2015). Contrary to this belief, research demonstrates that human memory does not simply replay past events as they occurred but instead engages in constructive processes involving emotions, schemas, fragments of memory, and guesses, leading to potential errors (Schacter, 2012). Memory's constructive nature enables the flexible simulation of future outcomes, such as episodic future event simulation, by allowing individuals to mentally construct and experience potential future situations (Schacter & Addis, 2007). However, memory errors can lead to inaccuracies in the simulation of future events, such as the planning fallacy, where individuals underestimate the time needed for tasks. This underestimation is due to reliance on incomplete or abstract features of anticipated scenarios, neglecting possible alternatives, and misjudging the reliability of the included information (Buehler et al., 2010).

Nonetheless, the same constructive processes that can lead to planning fallacies and misjudgments about future scenarios also provide a foundation for therapeutic techniques aimed at envisioning a healthier future. By tapping into this process, therapists have a unique opportunity to utilize memory's constructive qualities for healing. Those unable to form a coherent narrative of their trauma often store memories as disconnected somatic sensations or visual images, a condition that can manifest as symptoms of post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) (Van Der Kolk, 1998).

By utilizing this constructive nature as an adaptive feature, we can further empower individuals to mentally navigate and prepare for upcoming scenarios, thereby enhancing decision-making and the effective execution of behaviours. Future event simulations and techniques such as guided imagery can help patients integrate traumatic memories into coherent stories, by enabling individuals to envision overcoming past traumas in future scenarios. This methodological approach aids in therapeutic processes, allowing individuals to navigate future challenges with greater efficacy.
