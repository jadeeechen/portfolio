**Functional connectivity**, a fundamental concept in cognitive neuroscience, illustrates the intricate networks and their underlying cognitive processes, highlighting the crosstalk between brain regions rather than direct anatomical pathways (Pessoa, 2022). However, the reliability of functional connectivity studies can be compromised by the presence of noise in fMRI data, and if not properly controlled, spurious correlations may be confused for genuine functional interactions (Eickhoff & Müller, 2015). Furthermore, relying solely on a single measure, such as Pearson's correlation coefficient, may lead to an incomplete characterization of functional connectivity (Mohanty et al., 2020). The study emphasized that a low Pearson score doesn't indicate an absence of dependence between variables, but rather the lack of linear relationship, highlighting the importance of adopting diverse methodological approaches.

Insights from studies on functional connectivity suggest that the genetic contribution to network strength varies across cortical regions, exemplifying the importance of considering genetic foundations in understanding brain connectivity (Burger et al., 2022). In addition, findings provide evidence supporting the hypothesis that delayed functional communication between the salience network and default mode network is correlated with both positive and negative symptoms observed in schizophrenia, offering potential targets for therapeutic interventions (Hare et al., 2018).

These examples demonstrate how understanding functional connectivity, including its genetic basis and its association with mental disorders, can shed light on the underlying mechanisms of psychiatric disorders and inform therapeutic interventions tailored to individual patients. Thus, insights from studies on functional connectivity can extend beyond basic research and serve as significant implications for clinical neuroscience. Moreover, exploring the longitudinal effects of altered functional connectivity patterns in neurological disorders could provide valuable insights into disease progression and treatment efficacy over time. By utilizing diverse measures to identify genetic markers associated with altered connectivity patterns in neurological disorders, we can enhance diagnostic accuracy, leading to more effective interventions and improved patient outcomes.
