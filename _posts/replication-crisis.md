Psychology is currently perceived to be experiencing a **replication crisis**, as attempts to reproduce the results of previous studies often fail to yield the same outcomes (Maxwell et al., 2015). The push for external funding in academia exacerbates challenges like questionable research practices—such as the file-drawer effect, P-hacking, and HARKing—narrow research agendas, over-specialization leading to diminished creativity, overstated outcomes, and constrained opportunities for deep reflection (Page et al., 2021; Schooler, 2014). However, in addition to human errors and the process of scientific investigation, unstable contextual interactions between data and assumptions of statistical inference itself are a noteworthy concern. Amrhein et al. (2019) state that statistical tests often fail to be replicated because they're built on many uncertain assumptions, and the replication crisis highlights the need to see statistical results as temporary and uncertain. Thus, instead of merely emphasizing selective results, comprehensive disclosure of the research process, regardless of their statistical significance, becomes critical for advancing the reliability and integrity of scientific inquiry.

Given the outlined problems, metascience emerges as a promising approach due to its comprehensive analysis and refinement of scientific methodologies, targeting the core of the replication crisis by advocating for transparency and reliability in research findings (Schooler, 2014). However, while metascience is instrumental in refining research methodologies, it alone cannot fully address the replication crisis in psychology. The integration of psychological humanities is essential, as it addresses metascience's limitations by incorporating a wider range of perspectives, including ethical, theoretical, and historical insights (Newman et al., 2020).

To navigate beyond the replication crisis, psychology must embrace a broader perspective that includes both metascience and psychological humanities, moving beyond purely methodological fixes. By integrating these approaches, the discipline can foster a more comprehensive and refined framework, ultimately strengthening the reliability, validity, and integrity of psychological research.
